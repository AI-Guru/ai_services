How each method drafts

All three rely on the same target model for the final answer. They differ in how candidate tokens are proposed before the target validates them in a single forward pass.

Baseline — no speculation

Each target forward pass produces exactly one token. Decode is bandwidth-bound on all 27 B active parameters — ~1.6 TB/s ÷ 27 GB ≈ 60 tok/s theoretical ceiling.

target─►t₁ target─►t₂ target─►t₃ target─►t₄ … one full forward pass → one token

MTP — built-in autoregressive draft head

Qwen3.6's qwen3_next_mtp auxiliary head proposes the next token in roughly one extra attention layer's worth of compute. The target verifies and emits both in a single forward pass when the draft is accepted.

┌─draft head─►t₂̂ ┌─draft head─►t₄̂ │ │ target───┴─►t₁ + verify t₂ target───┴─►t₃ + verify t₄ one forward pass → one OR two tokens

DFlash — external block-diffusion drafter

A separate 2 B BF16 drafter z-lab/Qwen3.6-27B-DFlash performs a single non-autoregressive forward pass that proposes a block of up to 15 tokens at once via a diffusion step. The target then validates them in a single batched forward pass.

drafter ──► block of 15 candidate tokens (one diffusion step) │ ▼ target verifies all 15 in one batched forward pass │ ▼ emit 4-9 accepted tokens per cycle (typical)

Why DFlash wins

Decode on the dense 27B is memory-bandwidth-bound: each token requires streaming ~27 GB of FP8 active weights through tensor cores. Speculative decoding amortises that cost by emitting multiple tokens per target forward pass.

Config Avg tokens emitted per target forward pass Effective bandwidth utilisation
Baseline 1.0 ~78 %
MTP (draft=1) ~1.42 ~111 %
DFlash (draft=15) ~1.98 ~155 %

"Effective bandwidth utilisation" is measured tok/s ÷ theoretical ceiling (60 tok/s at 1.6 TB/s ÷ 27 GB). Values above 100 % are genuine — they mean we're emitting more tokens per model load than the raw bandwidth would allow, which is the whole point of speculation. Even at draft=15 DFlash's acceptance rate is ~13 % (2 of 15), but each accepted token is essentially free in bandwidth terms.

Why DFlash beats MTP here:
  • Parallel drafting. MTP's built-in head drafts one token at a time; DFlash drafts a block of 15 in one non-autoregressive forward pass.
  • Better acceptance on dense targets. On RTX PRO 6000 the dense 27B target's 21 ms/token forward pass is expensive, so every accepted draft saves meaningful time.
  • Z-lab reports 4–5× on B200. We see 2× here because B200 has ~3.4 TB/s bandwidth vs our 1.6 TB/s, plus newer FP8 kernels. The technique is the same; the delta is raw hardware.

The +14 % steady-state gain (avg +5.8 %: 214.7 vs 203 tok/s) is modest compared to DFlash's +98 % on the dense 27B, but it's a real win — and the first speculative method to give the MoE 35B any measurable speedup on this hardware. Quality smoke tests pass: coherent haiku, correct arithmetic on 17×23, developer-role pirate persona honored.

Why DFlash succeeds where MTP failed on the MoE:
  • External drafter, not a built-in head. MTP's draft head shares the MoE's expert-routing forward path; one drafted token costs almost as much as one verified token. DFlash's 0.5 B dense drafter has its own forward pass at ~1 ms — much cheaper than spawning the MoE for each draft token.
  • Block diffusion, not autoregressive. DFlash drafts 15 tokens in a single non-autoregressive pass; MTP drafts 1 token per head call.
  • Smaller gain than on dense 27B is expected. The MoE target's verify pass is cheap (~5 ms), so the bandwidth saved per accepted draft is smaller in absolute terms. The 27B target's forward pass is ~21 ms — every accepted draft saves roughly 4× more wall time.

Under concurrency: MTP vs DFlash on the 27B

Single-user benchmarks are flattering to DFlash. To see how each method actually behaves under load, we ran the full GuideLLM sweep against both configurations on the 27B (5 scenarios, 10 concurrency strategies each, ~75 min per sweep). The headline: DFlash dominates solo and at low concurrency, MTP catches up around C=8 and pulls ahead at high concurrency on chat workloads.

Conclusions from the full sweep:
  • Solo / low concurrency (C ≤ 6): DFlash wins, sometimes by a lot (+53 % chat at C=1, +63 % codegen).
  • Medium concurrency (C = 6–8): roughly tied. Per-request latency is the tiebreaker — DFlash gives faster individual responses, MTP shaves a few percent of aggregate.
  • High concurrency (C ≥ 10): MTP holds aggregate throughput better. DFlash's 15-token verify batches cause batching pressure that compounds.
  • Long prompts (RAG, summarization, agentic): both methods give only modest gains. Prefill dominates; decode speculation has limited impact regardless of method.
