Why the gap: dense 27B streams all 27 GB of active weights per token (decode bandwidth-bound at ~60 tok/s ceiling). The 35B MoE activates only ~3 GB per token, pushing the ceiling 9× higher and never actually hitting it in these scenarios. The MoE's ~200 tok/s single-user is ~3× the dense ceiling.

Why MTP doesn't help the MoE: speculative decoding trades extra forward-pass work for fewer total passes. On the MoE each forward pass is already cheap (~5 ms), so the draft + verify overhead eats most of the gain. Tested separately and committed as a negative result — the 35B FP8 configs do not enable MTP. The 27B FP8 configs do, and it's worth +41% there.

Findings

MoE is the throughput champion. Every scenario at C=1 runs at 165–210 tok/s — ~3× the dense 27B's 46–69 tok/s band. At saturating concurrency the gap widens to 3–9× on aggregate throughput.

Prefill is still the single-GPU ceiling. Long-prompt scenarios (RAG, agentic, summarization) cap at 1–4 concurrent users; decode capacity is never the binding constraint.

One RTX PRO 6000 serves roughly: 25–30 chat users · 12–15 coders · 8–10 RAG users · 4–5 agentic/summarization users. A 2–3× capacity improvement over the dense 27B.

Methodology

GuideLLM sweep profile per scenario: synchronous baseline (C=1), saturating throughput probe (C=512 nominal), then 8 constant-rate strategies interpolating between them. Each strategy runs for --max-seconds 120 with 10 % warmup discarded. Values are means across the steady-state window.

Scenario token distributions are sampled from normal distributions matched to production traffic (OpenRouter, Databricks traces); min/max clamped at mean ± 2 σ.
