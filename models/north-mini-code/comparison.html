Throughput read:
  • North-Mini-Code's fastest config (llama.cpp Q4, 278 tok/s) is the quickest no-spec number in the field — its ~17 GB Q4 weights win the bandwidth-bound decode of a 3B-active MoE.
  • It trails only configs with an edge it can't match yet: Qwen's NVFP4 + MTP speculative decoding (368), Nemotron Cascade-2's lighter AWQ-int4 (351), and Nemotron Nano's cheaper-to-decode Mamba-2 hybrid (296). North-Mini-Code ships no MTP head / drafter, so FP8 decode is its ceiling today.
  • On a true FP8-vs-FP8 footing it's mid-pack: 179 vs Qwen3.6-35B's 203.
  • The lone dense entry shows why MoE wins serving. Qwen3.6-27B (slate bars) is all-27B-active: at FP8 no-spec it manages just 47 tok/s — ~3.8× slower than North-Mini-Code's FP8 (179) despite similar total size, because decode is bandwidth-bound on 9× the active params. Even its INT4+DFlash champion (146) lands below the slowest MoE config here. Dense buys per-token quality (see the benchmark table) at a steep throughput price.

Caveat: cited numbers are each README's headline single-user figure; the exact prompt / harness varies slightly between families (default test_chat.py prompt vs test_scenarios.py chat scenario), so treat ±10% as noise. All on the same RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell.

Published benchmark quality — coding, agentic, math

Throughput is only half the story. North-Mini-Code is a coding / terminal-agent specialist; its size-peers here are generalists (Qwen3.6-35B-A3B MoE, Qwen3.6-27B dense, Gemma4-26B-A4B) or a math/code-reasoning specialist (Nemotron-Cascade-2). Qwen3.6-27B is the odd one out on form factor — a 27B dense model (all params active), included because it's in this repo and is Qwen's coding-strongest open weight. That shows up sharply in what each vendor chose to publish: Cohere reports agentic-coding numbers and almost nothing else; the generalists report math + science; Nemotron reports competition coding + math. Empty cells below are n/d — genuinely not disclosed for that model/variant, which is itself a finding.

Read the columns, not the rows — methodology is not uniform. The SWE-bench and Terminal-Bench numbers come from different harnesses and different pass@k: North-Mini-Code's 80.2 / 55.1 are pass@10 (SFT checkpoint, SWE-agent harness v1.1.0); Qwen3.6's 73.4 used Alibaba's internal scaffold; Nemotron's 50.2 used OpenHands; all likely nearer pass@1. pass@10 ≫ pass@1, so the agentic-coding column is not a strict ranking — treat it as "which league each model plays in."

What the benchmarks actually say:
  • North-Mini-Code owns its lane. On the two things Cohere built it for — SWE-bench-style agentic coding (80.2 p@10) and Terminal-Bench (55.1 p@10) — it tops this size class, and its AA Coding Index (33.4) is reported to beat models 4× its size (Nemotron-3-Super 120B, Devstral-2 123B at 72.2 SWE) and to sit within a point of Claude Opus 4.6 (80.8 SWE Verified). Caveat: that's pass@10 vs likely pass@1.
  • It deliberately skips the rest. Cohere published no AIME, GPQA, or MMLU-Pro — North-Mini-Code is a software-engineering agent, not a math/science generalist. If you need reasoning breadth, it's the wrong tool.
  • Nemotron-Cascade-2 is a code-reasoning champion, not an agent. It tops LiveCodeBench v6 (87.2) and AIME (92.4) — competition-style — yet collapses on real agentic work (SWE 50.2, Terminal-Bench 21.1). Great at solving a stated problem, weak at driving a repo/terminal. And it's the throughput winner (351 tok/s).
  • Qwen3.6-35B-A3B is the best all-rounder. Strong agentic coding (73.4), strong math (AIME 92.7), strong science (GPQA 86.0) — no single peak, no holes.
  • The dense Qwen3.6-27B out-codes its own 35B-A3B MoE — 77.2 vs 73.4 on SWE-bench, 59.3 vs 51.5 on Terminal-Bench, and the table's best GPQA (87.8). All 27B params fire per token (vs 3B for the MoE), so per-token quality is higher — but it's the slowest to serve here (dense decode is bandwidth-bound: ~47 tok/s FP8, ~146 with INT4+DFlash, far under the MoE field). It's the closest published-benchmark rival to North-Mini-Code on agentic coding, at a real throughput cost.
  • Gemma4-26B-A4B is the multimodal generalist. Solid across LiveCodeBench (77.1) / AIME (88.3) / GPQA (82.3), plus image+video input the others lack — but no published SWE-bench/Terminal-Bench, so its agentic-coding standing is unknown.
  • Throughput ≠ the quality config. North's headline 278 tok/s is the lossy Q4 GGUF; its 80.2 SWE number lives on the FP8/BF16 weights (179 tok/s here). Pick the row that matches your fidelity needs, not just the tallest bar.
